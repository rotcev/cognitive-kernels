## SECTION 1 — EMERGENCY FUND SIZING

### Target Amount Calculation

Using the industry-standard 3–6 month range on the $3,000–$5,000/month expense envelope:

| Coverage | @ $3,000/mo | @ $4,000/mo (midpoint) | @ $5,000/mo |
|----------|------------|----------------------|------------|
| **3 months (minimum)** | $9,000 | $12,000 | $15,000 |
| **4 months** | $12,000 | $16,000 | $20,000 |
| **5 months (recommended)** | $15,000 | $20,000 | $25,000 |
| **6 months (conservative max)** | $18,000 | $24,000 | $30,000 |

**Recommended target for this profile: $15,000–$20,000** (5 months at $3k–$4k/mo)
- Side business plans elevate risk to the upper end of the range
- Student debt servicing (~$132/month) must remain covered during income gaps
- 5-month buffer allows 3 months job search + 2 months business pivot time

### Why Emergency Fund Comes FIRST — Before Investing or Debt Payoff

1. **Forced liquidation risk**: Without an emergency fund, any unexpected expense (medical, car, job loss) forces you to sell investments — potentially at a loss during a market downturn. Sequence-of-returns risk is catastrophic on small portfolios.
2. **Debt payoff backfire**: Aggressively paying down student loans while holding zero liquid reserves means a $5,000 emergency reverses your payoff progress instantly — usually via high-interest credit card (19–29% APR), which is far worse than the 5.8% student loan.
3. **Business launch protection**: Side businesses require capital injections at unpredictable times. A depleted emergency fund forces you to raid investments or abandon the business at the critical moment.
4. **Psychological stability**: Research consistently shows that financial anxiety impairs decision-making. A funded emergency account removes the worst-case fear that derails long-term strategy.

### Form: High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)

- **Current HYSA rates**: ~4.5–5.0% APY (Marcus, Ally, Sofi, Discover, etc.)
- **Why HYSA over investing**: Sequence-of-returns risk = catastrophic. A $15,000 emergency fund in the S&P could be worth $10,500 during a bear market exactly when you need it.
- **Earning while waiting**: At 4.75% APY, $20,000 generates ~$950/year in interest — real, liquid, risk-free income while the rest of the portfolio compounds.
- **Do NOT**: CDs (lock-up), money market mutual funds (not FDIC insured), or bonds (interest rate risk on short-term needs).

## SECTION 2 — DEBT vs. INVEST MATH AT 5.8%

### The Break-Even Threshold

The **canonical rule of thumb**: Pay off debt above 6%; invest below 6%. At 5.8%, you are 0.2 percentage points below the historical break-even — the borderline zone where both strategies are mathematically defensible.

### After-Tax Adjusted Rates

Student loan interest is deductible up to $2,500/year (phase-out starts at $80k MAGI — you likely qualify at ~$60k salary):

- **Effective after-tax cost of 5.8% debt** = 5.8% × (1 − 0.22) = **4.52%**
  *(22% federal bracket, assuming standard deduction applies)*

- **After-tax S&P 500 return (taxable brokerage)**:
  - Gross nominal return: 9.0%
  - Long-term capital gains tax drag (15%): −1.35%
  - Dividend yield tax drag (~1.5% yield × 15%): −0.225%
  - **Net after-tax return: ~7.4%**

- **After-tax spread (investing advantage)**: 7.4% − 4.52% = **+2.88 percentage points**

### Guaranteed Return vs. Expected Return

| Strategy | Return | Certainty | Risk (σ) |
|----------|--------|-----------|----------|
| Pay off 5.8% debt | 5.80% (pre-tax) / 4.52% (after-tax) | **100% guaranteed** | 0% |
| S&P 500 index fund | 9.0% expected nominal | Probabilistic | ~16% annual std dev |
| HYSA (emergency fund) | 4.75% | ~100% (FDIC-insured) | 0% |

### Risk-Adjusted Expected Value Analysis

The Sharpe framework for the "invest instead of pay debt" incremental decision:

- Incremental expected return of investing over debt payoff (after-tax): 7.4% − 4.52% = **2.88%**
- Annual volatility of that incremental bet: **~16%** (full S&P 500 standard deviation)
- **Sharpe ratio of incremental bet**: 2.88% / 16% = **0.18**
- Historical market Sharpe ratio: ~0.35–0.40

An incremental Sharpe of 0.18 means you are being *under-compensated* relative to what markets have historically paid for bearing equity risk. The raw math says "invest wins," but risk-adjusted, the margin is thin and the standard Sharpe test suggests paying off debt is fractionally superior.

### Break-Even Risk Premium

To be indifferent on a risk-adjusted basis between paying off 5.8% debt and investing:

> **Required market return = Debt rate + Equity Risk Premium**
> = 5.8% + 4.0% (historical ERP over risk-free) = **9.8%**

- Historical S&P 500 nominal return: **9.0–9.5%**
- Gap from break-even: **−0.3% to −0.8%** below required premium

**Conclusion**: On a pure risk-adjusted basis, paying off the 5.8% loan is marginally preferable to investing in a taxable brokerage. However, the margin is small enough (~$1,600–$2,900 over 10 years per $12k) that personal risk tolerance, tax-advantaged accounts, and behavioral factors legitimately change the answer. The 401k's pre-tax compounding flips the math firmly in favor of investing before paying extra debt.

## SECTION 4 — OPPORTUNITY COST TABLE: PAY OFF LOANS vs. INVEST

**Setup**: $12,000 lump sum deployed two ways. Loan rate: 5.8%. Market return: 9.0% (S&P 500 historical nominal). Minimum monthly payment: $132.03 (10-year standard repayment, 5.8%/12 monthly rate).

### Path A: Use $12k to Pay Off Loan NOW
- Loan eliminated immediately; $0 interest accruing
- Free up $132/month from income → reinvest into S&P 500 at 9%

**Year 5 (Path A)**:
FV of $132/month × 60 months at 9% (monthly compound):
= $132 × [(1.0075^60 − 1) / 0.0075]
= $132 × [(1.5657 − 1) / 0.0075]
= $132 × 75.42 = **$9,956**
Loan balance: $0
**Net wealth from this $12k decision: +$9,956**

**Year 10 (Path A)**:
FV of $132/month × 120 months at 9%:
= $132 × [(1.0075^120 − 1) / 0.0075]
= $132 × [(2.4514 − 1) / 0.0075]
= $132 × 193.51 = **$25,543**
Loan balance: $0
**Net wealth from this $12k decision: +$25,543**

---

### Path B: Invest $12k in S&P 500, Continue Minimum Loan Payments

**Year 5 (Path B)**:
Investment: $12,000 × 1.09^5 = $12,000 × 1.5386 = **$18,463**
Remaining loan balance after 60 minimum payments:
= $12,000 × (1.004833^60) − $132.03 × [(1.004833^60 − 1) / 0.004833]
= $12,000 × 1.3355 − $132.03 × 69.41
= $16,026 − $9,162 = **$6,864 remaining**
**Net wealth: $18,463 − $6,864 = +$11,599**

**Year 10 (Path B)**:
Investment: $12,000 × 1.09^10 = $12,000 × 2.3674 = **$28,409**
Loan balance: $0 (fully amortized at 10-year term)
**Net wealth from this $12k decision: +$28,409**

---

### Opportunity Cost Summary Table

| Metric | Pay Off Loan (Path A) | Invest $12k (Path B) | **Winner** |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------|-----------|
| Year 5 net wealth | $9,956 | $11,599 | **Invest by +$1,643** |
| Year 10 net wealth | $25,543 | $28,409 | **Invest by +$2,866** |
| Certainty | 100% (guaranteed) | ~68% (market risk) | Pay off (risk-adjusted) |
| Break-even market return needed | — | **7.3%** | Investing needs ≥7.3% to win |

**Interpretation**: At the expected 9% return, investing wins by a *modest* $1,643 (Year 5) to $2,866 (Year 10). This is the prize for bearing 10 years of market volatility on a $12k position. If the market returns only 7.3% (one bad decade), both paths tie. Below 7.3%, debt payoff wins. Given the S&P 500's historical average, the mathematical edge slightly favors investing — but the margin is thin enough that personal risk tolerance is a legitimate tiebreaker.

## SECTION 5 — 401K MATCH ROI

### Immediate Return Calculation

**Assumptions**: $60,000 annual salary; 4% employer match; 22% federal tax bracket

| Component | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Your 4% payroll contribution | $2,400/year |
| Employer match (free money) | $2,400/year |
| Pre-tax tax savings (22% bracket) | $528/year |
| **Effective out-of-pocket cost** | **$1,872/year** ($2,400 − $528 tax savings) |
| **Total 401k value deposited** | **$4,800/year** (your $2,400 + employer $2,400) |

**Immediate return on effective cost**:
($4,800 − $1,872) / $1,872 = **156% return on first day**

This is why the 401k match is called "free money" — even before the money earns a single dollar of market return, you've already generated a 156% return on your effective out-of-pocket cost, due to the combined effect of the 100% employer match and the pre-tax tax savings.

### Why This Always Comes First — Regardless of Risk Tolerance

No other investment vehicle produces a guaranteed 100%+ first-day return. Paying off 5.8% debt, by comparison, generates a 5.8% guaranteed return. The employer match is **17× better** on a first-year basis.

### Long-Term Compounding Impact of the Match

Future Value of $2,400/year employer contribution over 35 years at 7% growth:
= $2,400 × [(1.07^35 − 1) / 0.07]
= $2,400 × [(10.676 − 1) / 0.07]
= $2,400 × 138.24
= **$331,776 in employer-contributed wealth**

**Cost of skipping the match**: Every year you don't contribute 4% to your 401k, you permanently forfeit $2,400 in employer contributions, which at 7% growth costs you $331,776 at retirement (35 years away).

Even one year of missed matching = $2,400 × 1.07^35 = **$25,622 permanently lost** from your retirement.

### Implementation Priority

- Contribution rate: Set to **minimum 4%** immediately (non-negotiable)
- Verify: Payroll match is deposited **per paycheck** (not year-end true-up, which carries vesting risk)
- Escalation: Increase to 10–15% within 2–3 years as salary grows (marginal cost decreases as income rises)
- Vehicle: Prefer **Roth 401k** if available (at age 30, tax-free growth over 35 years beats traditional for most income trajectories)

### Key Principles That Hold Across All Scenarios

1. **Emergency fund before everything** — it is the foundation that allows all other tiers to function
2. **401k match is non-negotiable** — 156% Day-1 return dominates all other uses of income
3. **IRA is always funded** — $7,000 into Roth IRA appears in all three scenarios; tax-free compounding at age 30 is too valuable to skip

5. **Business capital is risk-tiered** — conservative profiles treat it as a write-off; aggressive profiles treat it as primary growth vehicle
6. **Sequence matters more than allocation** — getting the waterfall order right (Tier 0 → Tier 7) generates more wealth than optimizing allocations within tiers
